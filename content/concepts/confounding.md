When we observe that two things change together, our brains naturally hunt for a story. If Harvard enrollment in online winter modules rises at the same time that library circulation drops, we might assume the online modules are drawing students away from physical study spaces. This is where we encounter the third variable: the confounding factor. A confounder is an invisible third element that influences both of the variables you are looking at, potentially creating a false sense of a direct relationship.

## The anatomy of a confounder

- The Observed Link: The apparent relationship between two variables, such as variable A and variable B, that seems intuitive or direct.
- The Hidden Driver: A third variable, C, that exerts influence on both A and B, acting as the true engine behind the movement you see.
- The Illusion: The false conclusion that A causes B, which occurs because we fail to account for how C pushes them both simultaneously.

In our library example, the third variable might be the academic calendar. During the weeks of intense module activity, perhaps the undergraduate population is primarily off-campus for winter break, leaving only a small cohort on site. The calendar dictates both the increase in online module participation and the decrease in library foot traffic. The modules are not causing the drop in library use; the timing of the term is responsible for both.

## Why this matters for Harvard staff

Harvard staff frequently analyze dashboards to understand student outcomes, alumni engagement, or departmental efficiency. If you notice that students who attend a specific wellness workshop have higher final exam scores, it is tempting to claim the workshop itself caused the performance bump. However, you must consider the confounder: perhaps highly motivated students are more likely to attend optional workshops in the first place. Their inherent motivation, not the workshop, is the third variable driving both higher attendance and better grades. Recognizing confounding variables protects you from implementing programs or policies based on correlations that are coincidental rather than causal.

## A reading example

Consider this excerpt from an internal memo regarding faculty usage of a new pedagogical software: "Our data shows that faculty who use the new digital suite three times per week report higher student satisfaction scores. To boost departmental ratings, we will mandate weekly usage of the software for all staff starting next semester." 

Before accepting this proposal, pause. Is the software actually driving satisfaction? Or is a third variable, such as 'faculty technical literacy' or 'smaller class size,' causing some instructors to be both more comfortable with new software and more effective in their teaching overall? If you mandate the tool without addressing the confounder, you may discover that forcing usage does not replicate the satisfaction scores you observed in your top-performing users.

## What to practice

Next time you review a report suggesting a clear link between two trends, use the claim-support-question routine to interrogate the findings:

1. Claim: What is the report suggesting causes this specific outcome?
2. Support: What evidence or data points suggest this is a direct relationship rather than a coincidental one?
3. Question: What hidden third variable could be influencing both of these trends at the same time?
