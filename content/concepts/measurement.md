# What was actually measured

In our daily work at Harvard, we often rely on data as a shorthand for the reality of campus life. When we say "student engagement is up" or "administrative efficiency has improved," we are using these terms as labels for complicated human experiences. Data fluency requires us to distinguish between the **construct**—the abstract idea we care about—and the **measure**—the specific data points we used to capture it. The gap between these two is where insight lives, and where bias often hides.

## The anatomy of measurement

- **The Construct:** This is the intangible concept you want to understand, such as "intellectual curiosity," "program quality," or "departmental climate." It exists in the real world but cannot be touched or directly counted.
- **The Proxy:** Since we cannot measure a construct directly, we pick a surrogate. We count course registrations as a proxy for engagement or response times as a proxy for efficiency. This proxy is what we actually track.
- **The Gap:** This is the distance between the construct and the proxy. A perfect measure would bridge this gap completely, but real-world data is rarely perfect. The gap represents the information we lose or distort when we reduce a complex experience to a single metric.

## Why this matters for Harvard staff

When we treat the proxy as if it were the construct, we risk "teaching to the test." If we define "advising success" strictly as the number of scheduled meetings, we might incentivize quantity over the quality of the interaction. If we define "community impact" solely through event attendance, we ignore the quiet, transformative work that happens in one-on-one relationships. As Harvard staff, our job is to interpret reports with a healthy dose of humility. We must recognize that every number on a dashboard is just a simplified version of a much deeper, more nuanced institutional reality.

## A reading example

Consider an internal memo from an advising office: "Our new outreach initiative was a success, as measured by an increase in digital resource downloads, which we are using as our primary indicator of student readiness."

Before taking this at face value, pause to consider the measurement. The office is using "downloads" (the proxy) to stand in for "student readiness" (the construct). It is entirely possible that more students downloaded resources, but that those resources were downloaded out of panic rather than proactive readiness. By relying on a single measure, the team has narrowed their view of student experience to what can be easily tracked on a server log.

## What to practice

Find a report, survey, or metric you use in your daily work. Use the "See-Think-Wonder" routine to probe the validity of the data before you incorporate it into your decision-making:

1. **See:** Look closely at the metric. What specifically is being measured? (e.g., "We are measuring email open rates.")
2. **Think:** What is this intended to tell us about our goal? What is the gap between that metric and the actual human experience? (e.g., "An open rate doesn't necessarily mean the content was understood or helpful.")
3. **Wonder:** What other information would I need to see to know if my goal is actually being met? What are we missing by relying solely on this number?
