## What shape your data takes

When we look at data, we often hunt for a single number to summarize a group. We ask for the "typical" time a student spends in the library or the "typical" class size. But a summary number is only half the story. To understand the reality behind that number, you must look at the shape of the data. This is called the distribution, and it reveals how your values are spread out across the range.

## The three common shapes

- **Symmetric:** Your data is balanced. Most values sit in the center, and the spread tapers off equally on both sides. Think of this like a classic bell curve where the mean and median are essentially in the same spot.
- **Skewed:** Your data is lopsided. A long "tail" of data points stretches out in one direction. If a few very high values pull the tail to the right, the distribution is skewed right, which often pulls the mean higher than the median.
- **Bimodal:** Your data has two distinct peaks. This often suggests you are actually looking at two different groups combined into one. For instance, if you combine data for early-career hires and senior leadership, you might see two distinct clusters rather than one central point.

In a symmetric group, a single summary number works quite well. In skewed or bimodal groups, a single summary number might be misleading, as it fails to capture the distinct clusters or the extreme outliers.

## Why this matters for Harvard staff

Staff members interact with data to make decisions about student services, event planning, and resource allocation. If you assume your student data is symmetric, you might build a program that works for the average student while failing the many individuals at either extreme. Recognizing shape helps you identify if you are looking at a single group of people or if you have inadvertently merged two different populations that should be analyzed separately. You do not need to perform complex analysis to improve your fluency; you simply need to move your eyes beyond the summary total to see the variety underneath.

## A reading example

Imagine a department head sends an update: "Our new mentorship pilot is a success, with a mean satisfaction score of 4 out of 5 across all 200 participants." 

Before accepting this as evidence of universal success, look closer. If you could see the distribution, you might discover it is bimodal. Half of the participants might have rated it a 5, while the other half rated it a 1 or 2, with very few in the middle. The "mean" of 4 is mathematically correct, but it masks the fact that the pilot polarized the group. It was a massive success for some and a failure for others. Reporting only the mean hides the underlying story.

## What to practice

Find a visualization or a list of numbers from your own office records. Look at the data and use the see-think-wonder routine to evaluate the shape:

1. **See:** What is the actual spread of these values? Are they clustered, spread out, or separated into two peaks?
2. **Think:** What does the shape tell me that the average cannot? Could there be two different groups hidden in this data?
3. **Wonder:** What would happen to my conclusions if I treated the outliers as a separate, important group instead of just noise?
