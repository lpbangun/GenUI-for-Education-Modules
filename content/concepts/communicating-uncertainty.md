### Saying you do not know without losing trust. In professional settings, we often feel pressure to deliver definitive answers. When you present data, there is a temptation to provide a single, rounded number as if it were an absolute fact. However, every data point carries a degree of uncertainty. Learning to communicate that uncertainty is not a sign of weakness; it is the hallmark of a data-fluent professional who respects the complexity of the information they handle. ### The three dimensions of certainty. First, there is the **source of variance**, which acknowledges that small samples or human behavior naturally fluctuate. Second, there is **the range**, which replaces a single point with a plausible interval. Third, there is **contextual framing**, which explains what the data cannot tell us. Instead of asserting that an enrollment project will reach exactly 500 students, you might report that, based on historical patterns, you expect between 460 and 540 enrollments. This honesty creates a boundary that protects your credibility while giving your stakeholders a realistic map of the landscape. ### Why this matters for Harvard staff. Whether you are crafting budget forecasts, reporting student satisfaction, or estimating event attendance, your stakeholders rely on you to signal what is settled and what is speculative. When you hide uncertainty, you strip your audience of the ability to plan for the full range of possibilities. By clearly stating the limits of your confidence, you invite your team to focus on the likely outcomes rather than chasing a specific, arbitrary digit. This fosters a culture of transparency where decision-making is resilient because it accounts for variation. ### A reading example. Imagine receiving a report from a departmental colleague: "Our survey indicates that 72 percent of students prefer the new scheduling pilot." If you look closer, the methodology section notes the response rate was low and the margin of error is substantial. A more transparent way to frame this would be: "While about 7 in 10 respondents in our small sample favored the pilot, the limited response rate means we should view this as a potential trend rather than a definitive consensus across the entire student body." The latter version maintains authority while accurately representing the quality of the evidence. ### What to practice. Take the next report you prepare or receive and apply the connect-extend-challenge routine to test its claims: 1. **Connect:** How does this data connect to what you already know about the underlying process or audience? 2. **Extend:** How does this range or uncertainty interval extend your current understanding of the situation, rather than just confirming what you hoped to see? 3. **Challenge:** What is the most significant challenge or limitation to the certainty of this claim, and how would you explain that limitation to a supervisor without losing their confidence?
