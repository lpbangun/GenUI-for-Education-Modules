Every dataset tells a story about who it includes and, by consequence, who it ignores. When we look at student feedback surveys, alumni participation rates, or committee sign-up lists, we often assume we are seeing a mirror of the whole population. Selection bias occurs when the method of gathering data favors some individuals over others, leading to a skewed version of reality. It is not about a lack of data; it is about the wrong kind of data being captured. Understanding this is essential to ensure our decision-making remains inclusive and grounded in the true experiences of our community. The problem is that once data is compiled, the omissions become invisible. We tend to trust the numbers in front of us while forgetting that those who did not show up in our records might have the most critical perspectives to share. The impact of this bias often goes unnoticed until a policy change leaves a significant group behind, simply because that group was not represented in the initial analysis. To identify selection bias, you must look beyond the numbers and interrogate the process that produced them. The goal is to determine if the data represents the diverse Harvard experience or just the voices of those most likely to be heard. Selection bias often presents in three primary forms: self-selection, where only those with strong opinions—positive or negative—take the time to respond; survivorship bias, where we only study those who made it through a process and forget those who dropped out; and under-coverage bias, where certain sub-groups are systematically less likely to be contacted or counted due to their communication preferences or institutional status. Recognizing these patterns helps you distinguish between meaningful trends and artifacts of how the data was assembled. Why this matters for Harvard staff is clear: your daily work involves using data to support students, faculty, and alumni. If your metrics on student satisfaction, for instance, only feature respondents who are already highly engaged in administrative processes, you might be missing the very students who require the most outreach. By identifying where selection bias exists, you can adjust your strategy to invite missing voices, creating more equitable programs. A reading example from a departmental summary: 'Our annual post-advising survey shows 90 percent student satisfaction across the board, proving that our new office hours model is a resounding success.' Before you accept this claim, notice three things: the survey was only sent to students who scheduled an appointment, it relies on self-selection from those who already use the service, and it ignores the students who never scheduled an appointment at all. This 'success' may only reflect the experience of the already-engaged. What to practice: find a common feedback or registration report in your office and apply the following routine: 1. Claim: What specific conclusion is this data trying to support about the entire group? 2. Support: What evidence exists that the respondents might be systematically different from those who did not participate? 3. Question: What specific group's perspective is currently missing from this data, and how would their feedback potentially change the result?
