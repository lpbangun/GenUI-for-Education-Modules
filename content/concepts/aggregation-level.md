### When the whole masks the parts

Data often reaches your desk as a summary. It is convenient to see a single figure representing performance across an entire school or administrative unit. However, aggregation—the process of combining data points into a single summary—often strips away essential context. When you look at an average of averages, you are often looking at a mathematical artifact that may not describe any single part of the whole.

### The three risks of aggregation

- **Weighting distortion:** If you average the success rates of five departments without considering how many students are in each, you treat a tiny program of two students with the same importance as a department of five hundred. This creates a misleading sense of scale.
- **The Simpson’s Paradox effect:** You might see a positive trend across a whole faculty, but when you look at individual departments, that trend could be negative. Aggregation can cause a result to appear in the combined data that contradicts what is actually happening in the sub-groups.
- **Loss of variation:** By collapsing many points into one, you lose the ability to see outliers. The extreme high and low performers effectively cancel each other out, leaving you with a comfortable, yet inaccurate, middle ground.

### Why this matters for Harvard staff

Harvard is a decentralized institution, and its data often reflects this complexity. If you work in admissions, financial aid, or student affairs, you likely rely on dashboards that summarize outcomes. When you see a high-level metric, such as 'average satisfaction across all undergraduates,' you are seeing a result that has been flattened. The risk is that this number may hide a persistent issue within a specific concentration or residential house. Staff members who only look at the 'top-line' number miss the opportunity to provide targeted support where it is actually needed. You do not need to perform complex analysis to recognize when a summary feels too simple to capture a diverse reality. Simply noticing that a report aggregates disparate groups is your cue to ask for more granular views.

### A reading example

Consider this line from a quarterly internal newsletter: 'Our satisfaction metrics across all regional professional development programs rose by 5 percent this year.' This sounds like a success, but what if the growth was driven entirely by one new, very small program while the larger, established programs actually saw a decrease in satisfaction? Because the report uses a single aggregated average, it masks a potential decline in core offerings. The headline celebrates a gain while concealing a significant loss in your most prominent programs. Without seeing the data broken down by individual site, the narrative of 'improvement' is incomplete and potentially dangerous for strategic planning.

### What to practice

Next time you encounter a summary statistic or a 'big picture' dashboard in your work, apply the claim-support-question routine to interrogate the aggregation:

1. **Claim:** What specific conclusion is this aggregated number asking me to draw about the overall program or department?
2. **Support:** What kind of underlying distribution or variation would I need to see to know this number isn't being driven by one or two extreme sub-groups?
3. **Question:** What specific, disaggregated view would tell me a different, perhaps more nuanced, story about our actual performance?
