# Who is actually in the data

When we review a report—whether it covers student satisfaction, faculty research support, or alumni engagement—we are almost never looking at the entire population. Instead, we are looking at a sample. Data fluency requires us to bridge the gap between the small slice of people represented in the data and the entire group we want to understand. If the sample does not reflect the diversity of the larger population, the findings can be misleading, no matter how sophisticated the analysis.

## The three pillars of representation

- **Selection:** This refers to how the participants were chosen. If you only survey students who frequent the library at midnight, your data will not reflect the needs of those who work full-time jobs or commute from off-campus.
- **Response Bias:** This happens when only people with strong opinions decide to reply. A survey on cafeteria food might be dominated by students who are either extremely angry or exceptionally pleased, while the majority who find the food acceptable remain silent.
- **Generalizability:** This is the leap we take when we assume that what happened with our small group is true for the entire Harvard community. It is a leap that requires careful verification.

## Why this matters for Harvard staff

Harvard staff frequently use data to justify program changes or resource allocation. If you receive a report claiming that "75 percent of students support a new scheduling policy," your first instinct should not be to support the policy, but to investigate the sample. Was this result based on a representative mix of undergraduates, graduate students, and residential populations? Or was it a quick poll sent to a mailing list that only reaches a specific department? When data is unrepresentative, it can lead to decisions that serve the most visible or vocal members of a community while leaving others behind. Recognizing the limits of your data helps you avoid the trap of assuming a part speaks for the whole.

## A reading example

Consider an internal memo from an advising office: "Our recent survey of 500 undergraduates shows that 80 percent of students feel 'highly satisfied' with the current pre-registration advising process." 

Before taking this at face value, look closer. If the office sent the survey only to students currently enrolled in a specific introductory course, the "500 students" are not a proxy for the entire College. Furthermore, if the response rate was low, you must ask: who chose to answer this, and who was too busy or too frustrated to participate? The reported satisfaction rate might reflect only the experience of those who found the process easy to navigate, ignoring students who encountered roadblocks.

## What to practice

Next time you encounter an aggregate summary or survey report in your department, apply the see-think-wonder routine to interrogate the sample:

1. **See:** Look at the data provided. What specifically is the sample? Where did it come from, and who specifically was asked to participate?
2. **Think:** What do you think about the representation here? Does this group look like the population you are responsible for, or is it missing key voices, demographics, or experiences?
3. **Wonder:** What do you wonder about the people who were not included in this data? How might their experiences differ from the ones reflected in these numbers?
