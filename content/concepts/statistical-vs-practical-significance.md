### Distinguishing detectable from meaningful

When you see a report claiming a program, policy, or intervention is successful, it often points to a P-value or a confidence interval. These are tools to determine statistical significance—or whether a finding likely occurred by chance. However, a result can be statistically solid while being practically invisible. Knowing the difference between what we can mathematically detect and what is actually worth the time of our students or colleagues is a cornerstone of data fluency.

## The three pillars of significance

- **Statistical Significance** measures the likelihood that a pattern is not just random noise. It tells you if the pattern is 'real' in the mathematical sense, but it is highly sensitive to the size of the group you are studying. With a large enough group, even tiny, meaningless differences become 'statistically significant.'
- **Practical Significance** asks if the difference is large enough to change how we act or how we support a student. It is about the magnitude of the impact. A change might be proven mathematically, but if it only shifts a final grade by a fraction of a percent, it may not warrant changing a curriculum.
- **The Relationship between Sample and Signal** describes how more data points (a larger sample) make it easier for statistics to confirm a tiny signal as 'real.' As samples grow, our power to detect small differences increases, but that does not inherently make those small differences more important to our daily work.

## Why this matters for Harvard staff

Harvard departments are often inundated with data. When a vendor presents software that 'significantly improves' student engagement, or an internal memo notes a 'statistically significant increase' in satisfaction scores, your role is to pause. If a change is so small that a student would not notice it, or so negligible that it does not alter their academic path, the statistical significance is a distraction. You do not need to check the math to evaluate the importance. You only need to assess whether the shift justifies the resource, time, or emotional investment required to implement the change.

## A reading example

Consider this memo from an academic support office: 'Our pilot study on the new library-booking interface yielded a statistically significant improvement in usage, with an average increase of 14 seconds per session compared to the previous portal.'

Before praising the upgrade, evaluate the claims. A 14-second improvement is statistically significant because the office tracked thousands of student logins, making it easy to confirm the difference isn't random. But is it practically significant? Does 14 seconds change a student's research experience? By anchoring your interpretation in practical utility rather than statistical confidence, you distinguish a genuine improvement from a mere blip on a chart.

## What to practice

When you encounter a report celebrating a statistically significant change, use the connect-extend-challenge routine to interrogate the impact:

1. **Connect:** How does this finding connect to what you already know about the student experience or administrative processes?
2. **Extend:** How does this finding extend your thinking about whether this initiative is worth the time or money it costs?
3. **Challenge:** What is the biggest challenge or drawback to trusting this 'significant' finding as a guide for your department's next steps?
