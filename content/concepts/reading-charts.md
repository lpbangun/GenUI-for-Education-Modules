### The narrative of the graph

When we look at a chart, our brains are wired to see a story. We see a rising line and assume success; we see a bar chart and compare heights without checking the foundation. But a chart is not a raw mirror of data—it is a translation. Every visual choice, from the scale of the axes to the colors used for comparison, acts as a filter that prioritizes one interpretation over another. Reading charts critically means moving beyond the visual shape and questioning the mechanics behind it.

## The three pillars of chart construction

- **The Axis Scale:** This determines the sensitivity of your view. A truncated axis, where the scale starts at 50 instead of zero, can make small differences look like massive shifts. It zooms in on variation but often strips away the context of the total value.
- **The Aggregation:** Data is often grouped before it is plotted. Are you looking at monthly totals, quarterly averages, or yearly snapshots? Changing the bin size—the way data is bundled—can hide noise or highlight trends that do not actually exist in the long run.
- **The Baseline Choice:** Every graph has a starting point. Sometimes a chart compares performance to a previous year, and sometimes it compares it to a theoretical goal. If the baseline is cherry-picked, the visual tells a story about relative performance that ignores absolute reality.

## Why this matters for Harvard staff

In our daily work—whether reviewing faculty enrollment trends, administrative workload dashboards, or giving campaigns—we encounter visual summaries constantly. A truncated bar chart might make a minor fluctuation in student interest look like a crisis or a breakthrough. If you accept the visual shape without looking at the axis scale, you risk reacting to noise rather than signals. Staff fluency requires recognizing that design choices are often intentional or, at the very least, biased by the creator's focus. By learning to interrogate the frame of a chart before the content, you ensure your decisions are based on the data, not the design.

## A reading example

Consider this blurb from an internal departmental newsletter: "As this chart shows, the number of student requests for advising has soared over the last three months, showing a dramatic 200 percent increase in demand." The accompanying bar chart shows a steep, vertical climb from May to July. However, upon closer inspection of the vertical axis, the scale starts at 1,000 and ends at 1,050. The "dramatic" soar is actually a shift from 1,010 to 1,030 requests. The visual is technically accurate, but it hides the fact that the volume is relatively stable.

## What to practice

The next time you encounter a chart that informs a decision, apply the connect-extend-challenge routine to move past the immediate visual impact:

1. **Connect:** How does this chart connect to what you already know about this data or process? Does the visual match your experience on the ground, or does it seem to tell a different story?
2. **Extend:** In what ways does this visual extend or broaden your understanding of the situation? What new questions does the chart raise that the creator might not have intended for you to ask?
3. **Challenge:** What is the biggest challenge to the chart's credibility? If you were to redraw this with a different axis, a different timeframe, or a different baseline, what part of the story would change?
