# What "typical" really means

When a report tells you "the average salary is $75,000" or "enrollment is up 12 percent on average," it is trying to give you one number that stands in for many. That one number is a measure of **central tendency**. There are three common ones, and they do not always agree.

## The three measures

- **Mean** is what most people call "the average." Add everything, divide by how many.
- **Median** is the middle value when you line everyone up in order. Half above, half below.
- **Mode** is the value that shows up most often.

In a group where everyone earns roughly similar salaries, these three numbers sit close together. In a group with a few very high or very low values, they can pull apart dramatically.

## The one question worth asking

Before you trust an average, ask: *is the mean representing the group, or a few loud members of it?*

A graduating cohort of 28 ed-policy masters students might have a mean salary of $93,400 and a median of $68,500. Both numbers are correct. They tell different stories. The mean includes one alum who took a CTO role at $500,000. The other 27 graduates cluster mostly between $58,000 and $85,000, with a handful of higher-earning industry and consulting roles above $100,000. When you are advising a current student on realistic expectations, the median is the more honest number.

## Why this matters for Harvard staff

You will read reports that choose which average to show. Sometimes the choice is principled. Sometimes it is persuasive. If a dashboard reports "mean giving per donor" but not the median, you are looking at a number that can be inflated by your largest gifts. If a program reports "mean time to degree" but the distribution is skewed by a handful of long stragglers, the median would give a more typical picture. You do not need to calculate anything to ask the right question.

## A reading example

From a hypothetical Division communication: "The Division reports an average giving rate of $4,300 per donor across 14,000 donors this fiscal year, a 7 percent increase over last year."

Before trusting the story this tells, notice three things: the word "average" (mean or median?), the absence of a median figure, and the fact that a few large gifts can move the mean considerably. A 7 percent increase in the mean might reflect broader donor engagement. It might also reflect one or two unusually large gifts.

## What to practice

Find a report or dashboard in your work that gives you an average. Before acting on it, answer three questions:

1. **Claim:** what is this number trying to tell me?
2. **Support:** what would the distribution need to look like for this number to be honest?
3. **Question:** what would the median tell me that the mean is hiding?
