### When data goes missing

When we look at a spreadsheet of student survey responses or donor engagement metrics, we often focus on the cells that contain numbers. We instinctively treat the blank cells as simple omissions or technical glitches. However, in data fluency, missing data is not an empty void; it is often the most significant part of the story. Understanding why data is missing is essential to understanding the reality behind the numbers.

## The three types of missingness

- **Missing Completely at Random (MCAR):** This is the ideal scenario where the gaps have no relationship to the data itself. It is like spilling coffee on a page; the missing entries are essentially random and do not bias your findings.
- **Missing at Random (MAR):** Here, the missingness relates to other recorded information. For example, if younger students are less likely to respond to a specific long-form feedback survey, their data is missing, but we can account for it because we know their age.
- **Missing Not at Random (MNAR):** This is the most critical type. The data is missing precisely because of the value it would have held. If students with the lowest GPAs decline to respond to an academic well-being survey, the gaps are systematic. The missing data is actively hiding the very trend you are trying to measure.

## Why this matters for Harvard staff

Whether you work in registrar reporting, development, or student life, you rarely deal with perfect datasets. When you see a high rate of non-response, you must avoid the urge to simply discard those rows and analyze what remains. If you ignore who is missing, you are creating a report that only speaks for the people who were willing or able to participate. By assuming that the missing group mirrors the group you have captured, you risk overlooking marginalized populations or specific subgroups whose needs are being masked by their silence. Data fluency requires you to pause and ask: who does this data represent, and whose voice is absent from this calculation?

## A reading example

Consider this line from a recent internal newsletter: "Our annual residential life survey results show that 95 percent of students are highly satisfied with their housing amenities, based on responses from 40 percent of the resident population." 

Before accepting this high satisfaction rate as the definitive departmental standard, consider the gap. The 60 percent of students who did not respond are not necessarily identical to those who did. If the students with the most complaints simply chose not to participate, the 95 percent figure is a reflection of a self-selected group, not the broader residential experience. The missing data acts as a filter, shifting the final percentage away from the true, aggregate reality.

## What to practice

Next time you review a report, a set of survey findings, or an administrative dashboard, do not skip over the notes regarding participation or response rates. Apply the following routine to interrogate the gaps:

1. **Claim:** What is the primary conclusion being drawn from this data?
2. **Support:** What evidence is provided to ensure that the individuals or entities that are missing do not hold views or characteristics that would change this conclusion?
3. **Question:** What could I do to investigate why the data is missing, and how might that change the story if I had access to those missing values?
