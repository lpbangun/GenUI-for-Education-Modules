## Plain-language definition
The denominator is the bottom portion of a fraction, representing the total group from which a subset or numerator is measured.

## What it tells you
The denominator sets the scope of your data. It tells you exactly how large the pool is before you calculate how many members of that pool meet a specific condition. Without knowing the denominator, a percentage or rate is essentially meaningless because you cannot gauge the significance of the numerator.

## When it matters
You must define the denominator clearly whenever you report rates, such as:
- Completion rates for mandatory training modules
- Survey response rates across departments
- Financial yields or student success proportions
- Error rates in administrative workflows

## Common confusion with the numerator
Confusion arises when stakeholders conflate the 'part' with the 'whole.' If a report shows that 50 students received a fellowship, but fails to clarify whether the denominator was the entire school or only those who applied, the resulting metric is misleading. Always verify if your denominator includes all potential candidates or only a self-selected group.

## Example in context
A department chair notes that '10% of our staff participated in the survey.' If the denominator is the entire administrative staff, the engagement is solid. However, if the denominator is only the subset of staff who opened the email invitation, the actual engagement rate is much lower than suggested by the percentage alone.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- Numerator is the upper number representing the portion of interest.
- Sample size is the count of items in your total dataset.
- Rate is the ratio of two related quantities expressed as a percentage or fraction.
- Cohort is the specific group that serves as the denominator for longitudinal studies.
