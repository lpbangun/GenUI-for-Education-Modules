## Plain-language definition

Comparing groups involves analyzing whether distinct subsets of data show meaningful differences in their characteristics, trends, or outcomes.

## What it tells you

Comparing groups allows you to move beyond aggregate numbers and surface nuanced insights. It helps you determine if a trend observed in the whole population—such as student satisfaction or faculty resource utilization—is consistent across all parts of the university or if it is being driven by a specific sub-population.

## When it matters

Use comparison techniques when you need to make evidence-based decisions for diverse audiences:

- Resource allocation: Ensuring support services reach the groups that need them most.
- Equity and inclusion: Identifying if specific populations are receiving different outcomes or experiences.
- Policy efficacy: Determining if a pilot program in one department achieved results that mirror those in another.
- Trend analysis: Seeing if changes in behavior, such as library usage, vary by academic level or affiliation.

## Common confusion with correlation

Comparing groups measures the difference between categories, while correlation measures how two variables move together. Finding that two groups differ is not the same as saying one caused the other; comparisons only highlight the existence of a gap, not necessarily why that gap exists.

## Example in context

A central office notes that total professional development hours at Harvard increased by 10%. By comparing groups—specifically by staff job family—the team discovers that the increase is concentrated entirely in administrative roles, while technical roles showed a slight decline. This comparison prevents a misleading conclusion that all staff groups are engaging more with professional development.

## Usage across Harvard

At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms

- Statistical Significance: Determines if the difference between groups is likely real or just random noise.
- Effect Size: Tells you the magnitude of the difference between groups, regardless of sample size.
- Cohort Analysis: A specific type of group comparison focusing on users who share common characteristics over time.
- Segmentation: The process of partitioning data into groups to make them easier to compare.
- Confidence Interval: Provides a range for the group mean, showing how much uncertainty is in your comparison.
