## Plain-language definition
A rate is a ratio that compares two quantities measured in different units, often representing a change over time or a proportional frequency.

## What it tells you
A rate provides context to raw numbers by grounding them in a denominator. While a raw 'count' tells you how many events happened, a 'rate' tells you how often they happen relative to a specific population, time frame, or resource. It transforms absolute figures into actionable metrics that allow for comparisons between different groups or time periods.

## When it matters
Use a rate whenever you need to normalize data to make fair comparisons. This is essential when groups vary in size or duration. For instance, measuring total office incidents is less useful than measuring an 'incident rate per 100 employees' because it controls for the size of the staff. Rates are the primary tool for identifying trends that are otherwise hidden by growth or contraction in the underlying population.

## Common confusion with ratio and count
People often use 'rate' and 'ratio' interchangeably, but a rate always implies a dimension of change (like time, distance, or per-unit basis). A count is just a total, while a rate is a count divided by some other variable. If you say 'the graduation rate,' you are identifying a subset of students relative to a total; if you just report the number of graduates, you are reporting a count.

## Example in context
A department chair notices an increase in the number of applications for a specific fellowship. While the raw count is higher than last year, the application rate—calculated as the ratio of applicants to total eligible students—has actually decreased. The rate reveals that the growth in applications is slower than the growth in the student body, suggesting a decline in overall engagement.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- Ratio: A comparison of two quantities, not necessarily involving a change over time.
- Percentage: A specific type of rate where the denominator is always 100.
- Frequency: The number of times a value occurs within a specific dataset.
- Denominator: The bottom number in a rate calculation; the basis for comparison.
- Trend: The long-term movement of a rate over time.
