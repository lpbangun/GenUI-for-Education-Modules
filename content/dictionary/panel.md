## Plain-language definition
A panel is a data set containing observations of the same subjects measured repeatedly over multiple points in time.

## What it tells you
A panel data set—often called longitudinal data—allows researchers to distinguish between changes over time and differences between individuals. By tracking the same subjects (people, organizations, or hardware), it reveals the dynamics of change rather than just a snapshot of a moment.

## When it matters
Use panel data when you need to answer questions about cause and effect or individual trajectories, such as: - Does a specific intervention actually improve student engagement over a year? - How do financial policies affect a specific set of organizations over a decade? - Do health outcomes change consistently for a patient group after a specific treatment? When you need to know *why* a total average changed, a panel shows you which specific individuals moved the needle.

## Common confusion with cross-sectional data
Cross-sectional data captures a 'slice' of everyone at one time, whereas panel data captures 'movies' of the same group over many times. If you compare the average GPA of seniors in 2020 to seniors in 2024, that is cross-sectional; if you track the same students from their sophomore year through graduation, that is a panel.

## Example in context
A research team tracks a group of 500 alumni over five years. Because they use a panel, they can see that while the average alumni salary remained stable, individual alumni actually saw a 10% increase in earnings while others took a temporary pay cut to pursue further education. A non-panel survey would have missed this individual-level volatility.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- Longitudinal data: The broad category of data that follows subjects over time. - Time series: A single subject measured repeatedly over time. - Cross-sectional data: A one-time survey of different subjects. - Attrition: The loss of subjects in a panel over time, which can bias results. - Balanced panel: A data set where no subjects are missing across the observation periods.
