## Plain-language definition
A longitudinal study tracks the same subjects or entities over multiple time points to observe changes or patterns throughout their lifespan.

## What it tells you
Longitudinal analysis reveals the 'how' and 'when' of change. Instead of just showing that group A is different from group B today, it shows the trajectory of an individual's journey. It helps separate short-term fluctuations from meaningful long-term trends, allowing researchers to infer causality more reliably than a one-time survey.

## When it matters
Use longitudinal methods when the goal is to:
- Measure growth or decline over time for the same population.
- Identify factors that predict specific future outcomes.
- Understand the sequence of events leading to a result.
- Reduce noise by comparing an individual to their own past performance rather than just to others.

## Common confusion with cross-sectional
Cross-sectional data is a snapshot at one moment; it is like a single frame of a movie. Longitudinal data is the entire movie. If you survey graduating seniors once about their career goals, that is cross-sectional. If you survey them every six months for five years, that is longitudinal.

## Example in context
An administrative team wants to evaluate a new advising program. A cross-sectional survey might show that students in the program are happier than those not in it, but that doesn't prove the program caused the happiness. By collecting longitudinal data on the same cohort of students throughout their four years, the team can control for baseline differences and see if individual happiness actually improves after enrolling in the program.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- Cross-sectional: A snapshot of a population at a single point in time.
- Time-series: Repeated observations of a single subject or variable over time.
- Panel data: Data tracking the same subjects (the 'panel') over multiple periods.
- Cohort: A group sharing a common characteristic tracked over time.
- Attrition: The loss of subjects over time, a common threat to longitudinal study validity.
