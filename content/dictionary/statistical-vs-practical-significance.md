## Plain-language definition
Statistical significance indicates a result is likely not due to chance, while practical significance measures if the effect size matters in real-world applications.

## What it tells you
Statistical significance confirms that a pattern observed in your data is unlikely to have occurred by random coincidence alone. However, with large enough sample sizes, even trivial differences can become statistically significant. Practical significance shifts the focus from 'is this result real?' to 'is this result useful?' by asking whether the magnitude of the difference actually warrants a change in strategy or behavior.

## When it matters
You must consider both when making decisions, specifically when:
- Analyzing large datasets where even microscopic differences become statistically significant.
- Evaluating whether a small performance boost in an academic or administrative process justifies the resources required to implement it.
- Reviewing experimental results to prevent overreacting to data that is mathematically 'real' but functionally irrelevant.

## Common confusion
Many assume that if a P-value is below the traditional threshold, the finding is automatically important. A result can be statistically rock-solid but practically meaningless if the effect size is too small to change a result. Never mistake a low P-value for a high-impact finding.

## Example in context
An IT department tests two different login page designs for a university portal. Because the portal has 20,000 daily users, the study detects a statistically significant improvement in load speed of 0.02 seconds. While the data suggests the change is 'real,' the team concludes the finding lacks practical significance, as no human user can perceive a 0.02-second difference, and the development cost is too high.

Across the University, these concepts are regularly applied in research grant proposals and institutional pilot evaluations to ensure that proposed initiatives are both evidence-based and impactful.

## Related terms
- **P-value** is the probability that observed data occurred by chance.
- **Effect Size** is the quantitative measure of the magnitude of the difference.
- **Confidence Interval** is the range within which the true value is likely to fall.
- **Power Analysis** is the calculation to determine if your sample size is large enough to detect a meaningful effect.
- **Type I Error** is the false positive of claiming an effect exists when it does not.
