## Plain-language definition

The **median** is the middle number when you order everything from lowest to highest. Half of the group sits above it, half sits below. For 28 graduates' salaries, the median is the average of the 14th and 15th salaries when you line them up. Ordering, no arithmetic.

## What it tells you

The median describes a typical case. Unlike the mean, it does not care how extreme the highest or lowest values are. One alum earning $500,000 does not move the median at all if most graduates earn around $65,000.

## When it matters more than the mean

Reach for the median whenever the distribution might be skewed:

- Salaries and compensation (almost always right-skewed by a few high earners)
- Donation amounts (a few large gifts distort the mean)
- Time to complete something, such as time to degree or time to response
- Home prices, rents, and most financial data in small samples
- Any distribution where one or two outliers can drag the mean away from the middle

## Common confusion with the mean

People use the word "average" to mean both things, often without knowing which. When you hear "the average is $75,000," you cannot tell whether it is the mean or the median until you ask. In skewed distributions, the two can differ by thousands of dollars or several percentage points.

## Example in context

A department reports that average salary among its 40 staff rose from $72,000 to $75,000 over a year. Sounds like broad raises. When the median is requested, it turns out it stayed flat at $68,000. The mean moved because two senior staff received large promotions. The typical staff member did not see a raise. Both numbers are true. The median tells the more honest story about what "typical" experienced.

## Usage across Harvard

At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms

- **Mean** is the arithmetic average. Use when the distribution is roughly symmetric.
- **Mode** is the most common value. Useful only for categorical data or discrete distributions.
- **Percentile** is the generalization of median. The median is the 50th percentile.
- **Outlier** is a value far from the rest. Medians ignore them. Means do not.
- **Skew** is the asymmetry of a distribution. In a right-skewed distribution, mean is greater than median.
