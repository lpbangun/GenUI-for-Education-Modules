## Plain-language definition
A cohort is a distinct group of individuals who share a specific defining characteristic or experience during a defined period of time.

## What it tells you
A cohort analysis allows you to track changes in a specific group's behavior or performance over time. By looking at a cohort rather than the entire population, you can control for external variables and isolate the effect of shared timing or common entry points into a program or system.

## When it matters
You should use a cohort-based approach whenever you need to understand the 'lifecycle' of a group. This is vital for: 
- Measuring student retention and completion rates across academic years.
- Analyzing the long-term impact of a specific intervention on a set of participants.
- Comparing the performance of different groups entering a system under different policies or conditions.
- Distinguishing between trends that affect everyone and those that only impact a specific group.

## Common confusion
People often confuse a 'cohort' with a 'sample.' While a sample is a subset of a population selected to represent the whole, a cohort is specifically defined by a shared experience or point in time, even if that group represents an entire population of interest.

## Example in context
An academic office notices that undergraduate GPAs seem to be rising. Instead of comparing all students across four years, they isolate the 2024 cohort. By tracking this specific group's data point-by-point, they discover that their growth was actually consistent, whereas the 'overall' increase was merely due to a larger percentage of seniors—who typically have higher grades—being enrolled that semester.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- Longitudinal study: tracking the same individuals repeatedly over a long duration.
- Control group: a group in an experiment that does not receive the intervention.
- Attrition: the loss of members from a cohort, which can bias long-term findings.
