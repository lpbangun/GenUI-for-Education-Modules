## Plain-language definition

Standard deviation measures how much the individual values in a set vary or spread out from the average value.

## What it tells you

A low standard deviation means most data points are clustered near the mean, indicating consistency. A high standard deviation means the data points are spread over a wider range, suggesting significant diversity or volatility. It provides a measure of reliability—if the deviation is high, the mean is less representative of the typical experience.

## When it matters

You should look at standard deviation when you need to know if an average is a reliable summary of the group:

- Assessing student grade distributions across multiple sections.
- Analyzing response times for shared services to identify if most users get a similar experience.
- Evaluating the consistency of energy usage in campus buildings across different seasons.
- Determining the risk profile of research grants based on historical spending patterns.

## Common confusion with standard error

People often confuse standard deviation with standard error. Standard deviation describes the spread of the data itself, whereas standard error describes the precision of the mean estimate. If you want to know how much individual salaries vary, use standard deviation. If you want to know how much your calculated mean salary might fluctuate if you took a new sample, use standard error.

## Example in context

An administrator reviews the time it takes for a department to process travel reimbursements. The mean is 10 days. If the standard deviation is 1 day, almost everyone is served within a predictable 9-11 day window. If the standard deviation is 5 days, some are reimbursed in 5 days while others wait 15, showing high inconsistency in service quality despite the same mean.

## Usage across Harvard

At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms

- Variance: The square of the standard deviation, often used in statistical models.
- Range: The simplest measure of spread, calculated as the difference between the maximum and minimum values.
- Normal distribution: A bell-shaped curve where the standard deviation dictates the width of the curve.
- Mean absolute deviation: The average distance of data points from the mean without squaring.
- Standard error: A measure of the statistical accuracy of an estimate.
