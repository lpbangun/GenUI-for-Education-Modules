## Plain-language definition
Variability measures how much the individual values in a dataset differ from one another and from the group average.

## What it tells you
Variability tells you how reliable a single number, like an average, is as a representation of a group. If variability is low, most points are clustered tightly around the middle, making the average a very strong descriptor. If variability is high, the data is spread far apart, suggesting that the average may not accurately describe the experience of any single individual in that group.

## When it matters
Use variability to understand context in situations where 'the average' is insufficient:
- Assessing the equity of salary distribution across a department.
- Evaluating student satisfaction scores where extreme negative or positive feedback might be hidden by a neutral mean.
- Determining if a change in policy had a uniform effect or if it helped some individuals while harming others.
- Analyzing resource allocation to ensure that capacity planning accounts for peaks in demand rather than just daily averages.

## Common confusion with error
People often treat high variability as a 'mistake' in data collection. While extreme noise can indicate measurement error, variability is often an inherent feature of human behavior or natural systems. You should treat it as an informative signal about the diversity of your data, not necessarily a flaw in your reporting.

## Example in context
Two departments both report an average faculty workload of 40 hours per week. In Department A, almost everyone works between 38 and 42 hours (low variability). In Department B, some faculty work 20 hours while others work 60 (high variability). If you only look at the average, you assume both environments are identical. If you analyze the variability, you realize that Department B is a high-risk environment for burnout regardless of the identical average.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- **Standard deviation**: The most common way to quantify variability by measuring distance from the mean.
- **Variance**: The average of the squared differences from the mean.
- **Range**: The simple distance between the lowest and highest values.
- **Interquartile range**: The spread of the middle 50 percent of the data, which ignores outliers.
- **Spread**: A non-technical, descriptive term for the degree of variability.
