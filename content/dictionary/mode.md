## Plain-language definition
The mode is the single most frequent value in a set of data. If you have a group of survey respondents, the mode is the answer that was chosen more than any other.

## What it tells you
The mode tells you about popularity and concentration. Unlike the mean or median, it is the only measure of central tendency that works for both numbers and categories. If you survey staff about their preferred work-location model, the mode tells you the dominant preference, even though you cannot calculate a 'mean' of 'remote' and 'hybrid.'

## When it matters
Use the mode when you need to know the 'most typical' or 'most popular' instance. It is indispensable for:
- Categorical or nominal data where numeric averages are impossible (e.g., job titles, departments, colors).
- Identifying peaks in a distribution (bimodal distributions have two distinct peaks).
- Understanding consensus or sentiment in qualitative-to-quantitative feedback.

## Common confusion with the mean
People often mistake the mode for the 'average' in a mathematical sense. If a test score has a mode of 90 but a mean of 60, it means many students scored high, but a few extremely low scores pulled the overall average down. The mode does not balance values like the mean; it simply counts them.

## Example in context
A survey of 100 staff finds that 45 prefer remote work, 40 prefer hybrid, and 15 prefer on-campus. The mode is 'remote.' Even if the mean of the 'hours spent in office' is higher than the mode might suggest, the mode correctly identifies the largest single group of stakeholders, which is crucial for planning office space.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, the mode is heavily used in distilling student feedback or course evaluations where the most frequent sentiment outweighs the mathematical average. In clinical contexts at HMS, researchers use the mode to describe the most frequent occurrence of adverse events. FAS and SEAS administrators often use the mode to identify the most common time slots requested by faculty for classroom scheduling, ensuring the most frequent needs are prioritized first.

## Related terms
- **Bimodal:** A distribution with two distinct peaks.
- **Frequency:** The number of times a value occurs.
- **Categorical Data:** Information that can be sorted into groups, where the mode is the primary way to find the 'middle.'
- **Central Tendency:** A set of measures including mean, median, and mode that describe the center of a data set.
