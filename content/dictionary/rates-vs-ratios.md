## Plain-language definition
A ratio compares two quantities of any kind, while a rate is a specific type of ratio comparing quantities with different units over time.

## What it tells you
A ratio tells you the relative size of two groups (e.g., 2:1), while a rate gives you a sense of speed or frequency (e.g., 5 enrollments per week). Ratios show magnitude relationships, whereas rates show how quickly something happens in a given interval.

## When it matters
Use ratios to communicate composition, such as staff-to-student support levels. Use rates to track performance and change, such as the rate of gift processing or the speed of IT ticket resolution. Confusing them can lead to misrepresenting a one-time snapshot as an ongoing trend.

## Common confusion with percentages
People often treat all ratios as percentages. While a percentage is a ratio of a part to a whole multiplied by 100, not all ratios are percentages. Rates are even more specific because they require a time-based or duration-based denominator, which a simple part-to-whole ratio lacks.

## Example in context
If a research center tracks 100 students and 50 faculty, the ratio is 2 students per faculty member. If the center adds 10 students each month, that is a rate of 10 students per month. Reporting the 2:1 as a rate would be incorrect because it lacks the time component.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- **Percentage**: A ratio where the denominator is always 100.
- **Proportion**: A ratio where the numerator is a subset of the denominator.
- **Denominator**: The bottom number in a ratio; the reference value.
- **Frequency**: How often an event occurs, which serves as the numerator in many rates.
- **Benchmark**: A reference point used to evaluate a current rate or ratio.
