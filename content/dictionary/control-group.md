## Plain-language definition
A control group is the baseline set of subjects in an experiment who do not receive the intervention or treatment being studied.

## What it tells you
The control group provides a 'business as usual' scenario. By comparing the outcomes of the group that received the intervention to those that did not, you can determine if the change was actually caused by your program or by outside factors like time or external trends.

## When it matters
Use a control group whenever you want to move beyond asking 'what happened?' and start asking 'did our intervention cause this?' It is essential when:
- Launching a pilot program where you need to prove efficacy before scaling.
- Evaluating the impact of a specific policy shift on student or employee behavior.
- Conducting A/B testing on web interfaces or departmental communications.

## Common confusion with comparison groups
While all control groups are comparison groups, not all comparison groups are controls. A control group implies random assignment or a highly specific, matched design. A generic comparison group might be flawed by selection bias—where the groups are different in ways that have nothing to do with your intervention.

## Example in context
An administrative office introduces a new student advising portal. They measure satisfaction scores for users. However, without a control group of students who did not use the portal, they cannot know if satisfaction increased due to the software or because it was a particularly smooth semester for everyone. A randomized control group would allow them to isolate the portal's specific impact.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- **Randomized Controlled Trial**: A study design where subjects are randomly assigned to a treatment or control group.
- **Baseline**: The starting measurement taken before the intervention begins.
- **Confounding Variable**: An outside factor that might explain differences between your groups, ruining the control.
- **Placebo**: A fake treatment used in control groups to account for the psychological impact of participating in a study.
- **Counterfactual**: The theoretical outcome of what would have happened to the intervention group had they not received the intervention.
