## Plain-language definition
A baseline is a known initial value or starting point used as a reference for measuring change or comparing future performance over time.

## What it tells you
The baseline provides the context of 'where we started.' Without this reference point, it is impossible to determine whether a change represents a significant improvement, a decline, or merely noise in the data. It isolates the effect of an intervention by acting as the constant state against which all subsequent activity is measured.

## When it matters
Use a baseline whenever you need to prove impact or track progress. It is essential when:
- Launching a new pilot program and needing to report on its success compared to the previous status quo.
- Monitoring energy consumption in campus buildings to assess the effectiveness of new efficiency upgrades.
- Analyzing faculty or student survey responses over multiple years to see if a policy shift actually moved the needle.
- Evaluating the performance of a new digital tool against the manual workflows that preceded it.

## Common confusion with targets
People often treat a baseline as a goal, but they are fundamentally different. A baseline is a factual report of where you are at the start; a target is where you want to be at the end. Confusing the two can lead to reports that claim success by failing to acknowledge that the starting point was already near the target.

## Example in context
An administrative office launches a new system for processing travel reimbursements. To measure if the new system is faster, they collect data for six months prior to the launch; this is their baseline of 12 business days per claim. After six months of using the new system, they find the average processing time is now 8 business days. The baseline of 12 allowed them to quantify a 33 percent efficiency gain.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- **Benchmark**: An external standard or point of reference against which things may be compared.
- **Control Group**: The group in an experiment that does not receive the intervention and serves as the baseline for comparison.
- **Variance**: The difference between actual performance and the baseline or expected outcome.
- **Longitudinal Study**: Research that tracks variables over time, requiring a clear baseline measurement.
- **KPI**: Key performance indicators often rely on baselines to define success milestones.
