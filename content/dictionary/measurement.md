## Plain-language definition

A measurement is the act of assigning a number to an observation or attribute of an object or event according to specific rules.

## What it tells you

A measurement provides a standardized representation of something qualitative or complex. It allows you to transform abstract concepts, such as student engagement or lab reaction rates, into numerical formats that can be compared, aggregated, or tracked over time.

## When it matters

Measurements matter whenever you need to ensure consistency. If you are comparing results across different departments or time periods, you must know how the underlying measurement was taken. A change in the instrument—such as switching survey platforms—often renders old and new measurements incompatible.

## Common confusion with data

People often treat data and measurements as synonyms. Data is the collective set of facts, while the measurement is the specific result of an observation or device at a single point. You have a dataset (the whole) composed of many individual measurements (the parts).

## Example in context

A registrar's office wants to track student attendance. Instead of relying on anecdotal reports, they implement a badge-swipe system. Each tap at a classroom door creates a measurement of time and location. The resulting stream of measurements allows the department to produce a report on facility utilization throughout the semester.

## Usage across Harvard

At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms

- **Metric**: A derived measurement or combination of measurements used to track progress.
- **Indicator**: A proxy measurement used to signal the state of a larger, often unobservable process.
- **Variable**: A label for the container where a specific measurement is stored.
- **Unit**: The standard scale used for a measurement, such as dollars or inches.
- **Standardization**: The process of aligning measurements to ensure they are comparable across different sources.
