## Plain-language definition

Sample size is the number of individual observations or data points included in a study, survey, or data set analysis.

## What it tells you

The sample size represents the scale of your evidence. It provides the foundation for determining how much confidence you can place in your results. A larger sample size generally reduces the margin of error and increases the likelihood that your findings represent the true underlying population, rather than mere random noise.

## When it matters

Consider sample size when evaluating the validity of any claim based on data:

- Before launching a survey, to determine if the number of expected respondents will be large enough to break down the data by sub-groups (like department or school).
- When reading research results, to check if the sample size is sufficient to support the conclusions being drawn.
- When analyzing small data sets, where a very low sample size makes outliers extremely influential and limits statistical significance.

## Common confusion with population size

People often confuse the sample size (the slice of data you have) with the population size (the entire group you want to understand). If you survey 500 staff members out of a total population of 5,000, your sample size is 500, not 5,000. Confusing the two can lead to overestimating the reliability of your data.

## Example in context

A department head wants to know if staff feel overworked. They send a survey to 200 employees and receive 20 responses. While 15 of those 20 report high stress, the sample size is only 20. Reporting that 75% of staff are stressed might be misleading because such a small sample may not represent the views of the other 180 employees who did not respond.

## Usage across Harvard

At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms

- **Statistical power** is the probability that a test will detect an effect given a specific sample size.
- **Representativeness** refers to how well the sample reflects the characteristics of the larger population.
- **Margin of error** quantifies the uncertainty in a survey result; it typically shrinks as sample size increases.
- **Population** is the entire group of individuals you are interested in researching.
- **Sampling frame** is the specific source or list from which the sample is selected.
