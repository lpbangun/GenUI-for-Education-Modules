## Plain-language definition

A percentage describes a relative rate of change, whereas percentage points describe the arithmetic difference between two distinct percentage values.

## What it tells you

These terms help you understand the scale of a shift. A percentage measures how much a value changes relative to its own starting point. A percentage point measures the absolute distance between two percentages on a scale. Confusing them can lead to massive errors in reporting the impact of programs or financial results.

## When it matters

Always use these carefully when describing:
- Annual budget growth or cuts.
- Changes in student demographics or yield rates.
- Shifts in research project success metrics.
- Any scenario where you are comparing two rates, proportions, or ratios.

## Common confusion

People frequently use the word "percent" to describe both situations. If a department's graduation rate moves from 90 percent to 95 percent, it is a 5 percentage point increase. However, that represents a 5.5 percent increase relative to the original 90 percent base. Using the wrong term makes your results look either vastly overblown or misleadingly small.

## Example in context

A university report notes that faculty participation in an equity workshop rose from 20 percent to 25 percent. A staff member writes that this is a "25 percent increase in participation." While mathematically correct (because 5 is 25 percent of 20), it is often more accurate and less confusing to call this a "5 percentage point increase." The latter emphasizes the absolute gap, which is usually the figure stakeholders need to understand the reach of the workshop.

## Usage across Harvard

At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms

- **Relative change** expresses the difference as a ratio of the original value.
- **Absolute difference** is the simple subtraction of two values.
- **Base value** is the original number used for comparison, essential for calculating percentages.
- **Rate** is a ratio describing a quantity per unit of measure, often expressed as a percentage.
