## Plain-language definition
The numerator is the top number in a fraction or the part of a ratio representing the specific subset being measured.

## What it tells you
The numerator defines the 'slice' of interest. Whether you are tracking the number of students who completed a survey or the amount of research funding allocated to a specific lab, the numerator isolates the specific variable you are trying to quantify relative to the whole.

## When it matters
You must define your numerator precisely whenever you are creating a performance metric or a KPI. An ambiguous numerator leads to misleading ratios. Always ensure the group described by the numerator is a subset of the group defined in the denominator.

## Common confusion with the denominator
The most common error is mismatching the scope of the numerator and the denominator. For example, if you count 'international students' (numerator) but compare them against 'all enrolled students including non-degree seekers' (denominator), your ratio will represent a different population than if you only included degree-seeking students. Always check that your numerator matches the logic of your denominator.

## Example in context
If a department reports that 50 students received a fellowship out of 200 enrolled students, the 50 is your numerator. It tells you exactly how many individuals met your criteria. If you only provide the numerator without the denominator, it is impossible to understand the scale or rate of that achievement.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- **Denominator** is the bottom number, representing the total group from which the numerator is drawn.
- **Ratio** is the relationship between two quantities, often expressed by dividing a numerator by a denominator.
- **Proportion** is a specific type of ratio where the numerator is included in the denominator.
- **Rate** is a ratio where the two components often have different units, such as 'dollars per hour'.
- **Subset** is a smaller set that is part of a larger, encompassing set.
