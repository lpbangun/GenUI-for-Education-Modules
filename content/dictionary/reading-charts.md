## Plain-language definition
Reading charts is the practice of systematically interpreting visual data representations to identify trends, relationships, and deviations from expected norms.

## What it tells you
Effective chart reading allows you to translate visual cues—such as slope, color density, and gaps—into actionable information. It moves a viewer from simply observing a plot to understanding the underlying story, revealing whether a value is increasing, stagnant, or anomalous compared to historical benchmarks.

## When it matters
Use chart reading skills whenever you are presented with dashboards or summary reports. It is critical when: 
- Comparing current enrollment or spending against historical targets.
- Identifying the onset of a trend before it becomes a formal issue.
- Evaluating the impact of policy changes by inspecting before-and-after visualization cohorts.
- Determining if an outlier in a chart represents a data entry error or a significant institutional event.

## Common confusion with skimming graphs
Many staff members confuse glancing at a chart with reading it. Skimming involves identifying the overall shape, while reading involves checking the axes, understanding the scale, identifying the data source, and verifying the units of measurement. Failing to read the labels often leads to misinterpreting a chart's magnitude.

## Example in context
An administrator reviews a line chart showing annual utility costs. A superficial glance suggests costs are rising. By reading the chart's axes and noting the inclusion of a one-time capital investment project in 2022, they realize the core operational expenses have actually remained stable. This interpretation changes the focus of the budget proposal meeting from cost-cutting to project justification.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- **Data visualization** is the act of creating the graphical representation.
- **Dashboard** is the interface that houses multiple related charts.
- **Trend analysis** is the deeper investigation into why the visual lines are moving in a specific direction.
- **Key performance indicator** is the specific metric often showcased as the primary focus of a chart.
- **Data literacy** is the overarching capability to perform tasks like reading charts effectively.
