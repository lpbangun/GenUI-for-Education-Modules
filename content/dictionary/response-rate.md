## Plain-language definition
The response rate is the percentage of total invited participants who actually completed a survey or provided a requested data point.

## What it tells you
The response rate measures how representative your data might be. A high response rate generally increases confidence that the results reflect the opinions of the entire group. A low response rate warns you that the respondents might have significantly different viewpoints than those who chose not to participate.

## When it matters
You should evaluate response rates whenever you rely on survey data to make policy or resource decisions. If you invite 1,000 staff members to a survey but only 50 respond, your response rate is 5 percent. At that level, you cannot reliably claim to know how the other 950 staff feel, as the responders may only represent a small, vocal subgroup.

## Common confusion with completion rate
A common error is conflating the two. A response rate is calculated by dividing the number of completed responses by the total number of people invited to the survey. A completion rate often refers only to those who started the survey and finished it, ignoring those who never opened the invitation at all. 

## Example in context
An administrative office sends a satisfaction survey to 2,000 faculty members regarding new software. They receive 300 responses. With a 15 percent response rate, the office must be cautious about reporting these as representative findings, as the faculty who felt strongly enough to respond—either very positively or very negatively—are likely overrepresented compared to the silent majority.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- **Sample bias** occurs when the sample does not reflect the target population.
- **Non-response error** is the bias introduced when those who do not respond differ from those who do.
- **Survey methodology** covers the design and delivery systems that influence response rates.
- **Confidence interval** quantifies how much uncertainty remains in your data due to sample size.
- **Participation rate** refers to the broader engagement level of an audience across activities.
