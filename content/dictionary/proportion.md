## Plain-language definition

A proportion represents the size of a specific part relative to the whole, expressed as a fraction, decimal, or percentage.

## What it tells you

A proportion converts raw counts into a standardized scale. It allows you to compare the magnitude of a subset regardless of the total population size. If you see that 20 students out of 100 failed an exam, the proportion is 0.20 or 20 percent. This standardized figure is essential when comparing performance across groups of different sizes.

## When it matters

Use proportions when you want to communicate:
- Representation: How many people in a group possess a certain trait.
- Participation: The reach of a program relative to the total eligible population.
- Efficiency: The successful completion rate of tasks within a workflow.
- Composition: How different categories combine to form a full budget or cohort.

## Common confusion with rates and ratios

A proportion is a part-to-whole relationship where the numerator must be included in the denominator. A 'ratio' compares any two quantities, such as students to faculty, which does not have to be a part-to-whole relationship. A 'rate' typically involves a change over time or an occurrence relative to an exposure duration, such as incidents per 1,000 person-years.

## Example in context

If a department reports that 500 students took a seminar, and 100 finished the optional final project, the proportion is 0.20. Reporting the raw count of 100 might sound impressive, but the proportion reveals that 80 percent of the cohort opted out. The proportion provides the necessary context to evaluate engagement levels accurately.

## Usage across Harvard

At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- **Percentage**: A proportion expressed as a fraction of 100.
- **Rate**: A measure of frequency or change, usually involving a time component.
- **Ratio**: The quantitative relationship between two amounts showing the number of times one value contains or is contained within the other.
- **Confidence Interval**: A range of values derived from a proportion that indicates the reliability of the estimate.
