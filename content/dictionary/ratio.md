## Plain-language definition
A ratio is a comparative statement of two quantities, expressing the relative size of one value to another through division. It captures the relationship between parts, rather than just the raw totals.

## What it tells you
A ratio tells you how much of one thing exists in relation to another. It provides a normalized view, allowing you to compare groups of different sizes. For instance, a 2:1 student-to-advisor ratio explains the level of individual attention available regardless of whether the department has ten students or one hundred.

## When it matters
Use ratios whenever you need to normalize data for comparison:
- Comparing staffing levels across different-sized departments.
- Assessing financial health through liquidity or debt metrics.
- Monitoring operational efficiency, such as tickets resolved per staff hour.
- Measuring diversity demographics where relative representation matters more than absolute counts.

## Common confusion with percentages
Ratios and percentages are siblings but not identical. A ratio (e.g., 1:4) expresses a part-to-part or part-to-whole relationship, whereas a percentage is always a specific form of a part-to-whole ratio scaled to 100. Confusing a 1:4 ratio with 25% is common; remember that 1:4 means there is one part for every four of another, implying the first part is 20% of the total five parts.

## Example in context
An administrator wants to show that a program is growing. Simply stating 'we added 50 students' sounds impressive, but it ignores that the program also doubled its faculty. By calculating the student-to-faculty ratio, the administrator can show whether the growth resulted in smaller class sizes or if the original capacity was simply stretched further.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- Proportion: A specific type of ratio where the part is compared to the whole.
- Rate: A ratio that compares two different units, such as miles per hour or students per term.
- Percentage: A fraction expressed as a portion of 100.
- Denominator: The bottom number in a ratio, representing the reference unit.
- Scaling: The process of adjusting values while keeping the underlying ratio constant.
