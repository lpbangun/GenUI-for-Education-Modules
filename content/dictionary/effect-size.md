## Plain-language definition
Effect size measures the magnitude of a difference between two groups or the strength of a relationship between variables.

## What it tells you
While statistical significance tells you if an effect is likely 'real' rather than a result of random chance, effect size tells you if the effect matters in the real world. A tiny change can be statistically significant if your sample size is massive, but an effect size calculation helps you gauge if that change is worth the effort, cost, or time required to implement it.

## When it matters
Use effect size when you need to weigh the practical importance of your findings against theoretical probability. It is critical when:
- Evaluating whether a pilot program's results justify a wider university rollout.
- Comparing the impact of different instructional methods across disparate study populations.
- Translating research results into actionable policy or administrative changes.
- Conducting meta-analyses to aggregate findings from multiple independent studies.

## Common confusion with statistical significance
People often conflate 'significant' with 'large.' Statistical significance is purely a statement about the likelihood of your data appearing if there were actually no effect; it is heavily influenced by the number of observations. Effect size is independent of sample size and focuses solely on the distance or strength between groups.

## Example in context
An administrator reviews a student-support intervention where the experimental group showed 'significant' improvements. While the math confirmed the result was not random, the calculated effect size was extremely small, suggesting the program change had a negligible impact on actual student performance despite the statistical victory.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- **p-value** is the probability that the observed results occurred by chance.
- **Statistical significance** indicates an effect is likely not due to random error.
- **Confidence interval** provides a range of values within which the true effect size is likely to fall.
- **Meta-analysis** synthesizes multiple studies using effect size as a common metric.
- **Cohen's d** is a popular specific formula for measuring the standardized difference between two means.
