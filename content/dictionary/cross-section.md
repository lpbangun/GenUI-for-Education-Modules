## Plain-language definition
A cross-section captures a single snapshot of data for multiple subjects at one specific point in time.

## What it tells you
A cross-section provides a 'slice' of reality. It shows you the state of a group—such as students, donors, or equipment—at a frozen moment. By looking across these different subjects simultaneously, you can identify patterns, gaps, or correlations without worrying about how those subjects changed over the previous months or years.

## When it matters
Use cross-sectional data when you need to know 'how things look right now.' It is the gold standard for:
- Point-in-time compliance reports
- Salary benchmarking across departments during a single budget year
- Sentiment analysis from a one-time campus climate survey
- Snapshot headcount reporting for enrollment management

## Common confusion with longitudinal data
People often confuse cross-sections with longitudinal studies. A cross-section is like a photograph: it shows everyone at once but doesn't show how they got there. A longitudinal study is like a movie: it tracks the same individuals over time to see how their values change. If you want to know if specific student habits cause higher GPA, a cross-section might suggest a relationship, but it cannot prove that the habits caused the outcome over time.

## Example in context
An administrator wants to know if there is a gap in professional development participation across Harvard schools. They pull a data export of all staff who participated in a workshop during the Spring 2024 semester. This list is a cross-section. It tells them who participated at that moment, but it does not tell them if those same people were inactive in the previous year or if participation trends are rising or falling.

## Usage across Harvard
At HGSE and HBS, outcome reports for graduating cohorts typically lead with the median salary because graduate outcomes often skew right. In clinical research at HMS, median survival time is the standard summary for time-to-event data because the mean can be undefined when patients are still alive at study end. Institutional research reports on giving or compensation at the central level generally include both, though the median is the more informative number in both cases.

## Related terms
- Longitudinal study is tracking the same subjects over multiple time points.
- Time series tracks a single subject over multiple time points.
- Snapshot refers to the static nature of the data retrieved at a specific timestamp.
- Cohort study involves observing a specific group that shares a defining characteristic over time.
